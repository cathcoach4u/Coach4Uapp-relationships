Relationships

Your Relationship Toolkit

A practical guide to understanding, strengthening, and growing your relationship together.

This toolkit is designed to support you and your partner as you work through your relationship journey. It brings together practical tools, reflection prompts, and research-backed approaches that you can use between sessions and beyond.

You do not need to work through everything at once. Use what feels relevant to where you are right now, and come back to other sections when the time is right.

Chapter One

Understanding Your Relationship Dynamics

Every relationship has patterns. Some are helpful, others keep you stuck in the same cycles. This chapter helps you notice what is happening beneath the surface so you can respond differently.

Pursuer and Avoider Dynamics

In many relationships, one partner tends to pursue connection (moving towards, seeking reassurance, wanting to talk things through) while the other tends to withdraw (pulling away, needing space, going quiet). Neither is wrong. They are different ways of managing the same underlying need for safety.

  • The pursuer often fears abandonment or being unimportant. They reach out more when they feel disconnected.
  • The avoider often fears being overwhelmed or criticised. They pull back to protect themselves.

The challenge is that the more one pursues, the more the other withdraws, and the cycle repeats.

Try this

Next time you notice this pattern, pause and name it together: "I think we might be in our cycle right now." This simple observation can interrupt the pattern and create space to respond differently.

Transactional Analysis: Parent, Child, and Adult

We all carry different "ego states" from Transactional Analysis. Sometimes we speak from the Parent (directing, correcting, nurturing), sometimes from the Child (reacting emotionally, feeling small, being playful), and sometimes from the Adult (calm, logical, present).

The most effective communication happens when both partners are in their Adult state. When one partner shifts into Parent and the other into Child, conversations can become unbalanced.

Try this

Before a difficult conversation, check in: "Am I speaking from my Adult right now?" If you notice a Parent or Child tone creeping in, take a breath and reset.

Staying Present: Past, Future, and Now

Conflict often pulls us into the past ("You always...") or the future ("This will never change..."). Gestalt awareness encourages staying in the present moment, noticing what is actually happening right now rather than responding to old stories.

"The relationship you want is built one present moment at a time, not by fixing the past or worrying about the future."
Chapter Two

Building Connection through Communication

Communication is the foundation of connection. This chapter offers practical approaches to help you talk with, rather than at, each other.

Communication Foundations

Good communication does not mean agreeing on everything. It means both partners feel heard, respected, and safe to share what matters to them.

  • Slow down. Pause before responding. Speed breeds reactivity.
  • Listen to understand, not to reply. Let your partner finish before formulating your response.
  • Name your feeling, not your judgement. "I feel worried" lands differently to "You never listen."
  • Use "I" statements. Start with your experience rather than an accusation.
  • Handle transitions gently. Reunions after work and bedtime are sensitive moments. A soft approach sets the tone.

Structured Conversations: Imago Dialogue

When emotions are high, a structured approach can help. Imago Dialogue uses three steps to create safety:

  • Mirroring: Repeat back what your partner said in your own words. "What I hear you saying is..." This shows you are listening without adding interpretation.
  • Validation: Acknowledge their perspective makes sense from their point of view. "That makes sense because..." You do not have to agree to validate.
  • Empathy: Imagine what your partner might be feeling. "I imagine you might be feeling..." This deepens emotional connection.
Try this

Choose a low-stakes topic and practise the three steps together. Take turns being the speaker and the listener. Notice how it feels different from your usual conversations.

Expressing and Understanding Emotions

Many of us were not taught how to talk about feelings. In relationships, unspoken emotions build up and come out sideways, as criticism, withdrawal, or resentment.

Start by building a shared vocabulary for emotions. Beyond "fine" or "angry," try naming specific feelings: disappointed, anxious, lonely, grateful, hopeful, overwhelmed. The more precisely you can name what you feel, the easier it is for your partner to understand and respond.

"Connection grows when both partners feel safe enough to say what is true, not just what is easy."
Chapter Three

Leveraging Your Strengths as a Couple

Strengths-based coaching focuses on what is working in your relationship, not just what is broken. When you notice and build on your strengths, you create a more resilient foundation.

Identifying Your Strengths

Each person brings unique strengths to the relationship. These might include empathy, humour, loyalty, problem-solving, patience, creativity, or resilience. Formal tools like the Gallup CliftonStrengths assessment can help, but you can also start with a simple question:

  • What do I do well in this relationship?
  • What does my partner do well?
  • What are we strong at together?

Strengths in Action

Once you know your strengths, you can use them intentionally:

  • During conflict: If one partner's strength is patience, they can take the lead in slowing things down. If the other's strength is directness, they can help name the real issue.
  • In daily life: Divide responsibilities in ways that play to each person's strengths rather than defaulting to old habits.
  • In repair: After a rupture, lean on your strengths to find your way back. If humour is your thing, use it. If writing is easier than talking, write a note.
Try this

Each partner writes down three strengths they see in the other person and shares them. Then discuss: how could we use these strengths more intentionally this week?

Strengths-Based Conversations

Make it a habit to notice and name what your partner does well. This is not about ignoring problems. It is about building a culture of appreciation alongside honest feedback.

  • "I noticed you really listened to me tonight. That meant a lot."
  • "You handled that situation with the kids so calmly. I appreciate that."
  • "One of the things I value about us is how we can laugh even when things are hard."
"What you focus on grows. When you notice strengths, you create more of them."
Chapter Four

Navigating Relationship Challenges

All relationships face challenges. What matters is not whether difficulties arise, but how you move through them together.

Managing Daily Stress and Chaos

Work pressures, children, finances, health, and the sheer pace of daily life can erode connection. The relationship often gets the leftovers of your energy.

  • Protect transition moments. How you greet each other after work and how you say goodnight matter more than you think.
  • Create small rituals. A morning coffee together, a two-minute check-in before bed, or a weekly walk can anchor your connection.
  • Lower the bar on hard days. When life is chaotic, even a brief "I see you and I am here" is enough.

Recognising Negative Patterns: Gottman's Four Horsemen

Research by Dr John Gottman identified four communication patterns that predict relationship distress:

  • Criticism: Attacking your partner's character rather than raising a specific concern. Antidote: Use a gentle start-up. Begin with "I feel..." rather than "You always..."
  • Defensiveness: Deflecting responsibility or making excuses. Antidote: Accept even a small part of responsibility. "You are right, I could have handled that better."
  • Contempt: Disrespect, sarcasm, eye-rolling, or mocking. This is the most damaging pattern. Antidote: Build a culture of appreciation and respect.
  • Stonewalling: Shutting down and withdrawing from the conversation. Antidote: Take a planned break. "I need 20 minutes to calm down, then I will come back."
Try this

Together, identify which of the four horsemen shows up most in your relationship. Agree on one antidote you will both practise this week.

External Stressors and Your Relationship

Sometimes the problem is not between you. Work stress, family pressures, health issues, or financial strain can spill into the relationship. The key is to face these as a team rather than letting them drive a wedge.

  • Name the external stress openly: "Work has been really intense this week."
  • Ask what your partner needs: "How can I support you right now?"
  • Avoid taking stress-driven behaviour personally when possible.

Understanding Each Other's Core Needs

Beneath most relationship conflicts are unmet needs: for safety, belonging, autonomy, respect, or recognition. When you understand what your partner truly needs (not just what they are asking for on the surface), you can respond with more empathy and accuracy.

Try this

Each partner completes this sentence: "What I need most in our relationship right now is..." Share without commentary or problem-solving. Just listen.

Chapter Five

Growing Together

A strong relationship is not a destination. It is a practice. This chapter is about building habits that keep your connection alive over time.

Regular Relationship Check-Ins

Set aside time each week to check in with each other. This does not need to be a heavy conversation. A simple structure works well:

  • Appreciation: Share one thing you appreciated about your partner this week.
  • Temperature check: On a scale of 1 to 10, how connected did you feel this week?
  • One request: Is there one thing that would help you feel more connected next week?

Keeping it brief and regular prevents small issues from becoming big ones.

Reflective Practices

Reflection helps you notice patterns, celebrate progress, and stay intentional about your relationship.

  • Journalling: Write briefly about how the relationship felt this week. What went well? What was hard? What do you want more of?
  • Shared reflection: At the end of each month, look back together. What did you handle well? What would you do differently?
  • Celebrate growth: It is easy to focus on what is still hard. Make space to notice how far you have come.
Try this

Pick a regular time for your weekly check-in and put it in the calendar. Treat it like any other important appointment. Even 15 minutes makes a difference.

"The strongest relationships are not the ones without problems. They are the ones where both partners keep choosing to show up, learn, and grow together."
