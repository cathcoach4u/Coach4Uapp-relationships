Your Attachment Needs

Understanding the needs beneath the behaviour, and how to ask for what you need in a way that can be heard.

Needs under pressure
What it looks like when distressed

Escalated expressions

  • Repeated questioning: Do you even care about me?
  • Accusations framed as questions: Why do you always do this?
  • Demanding comfort through criticism or blame
  • Withdrawing completely to see if they will follow
  • Escalating intensity to force a response
  • Saying hurtful things to provoke connection
This is not manipulation. It is a very human response to feeling unsafe or unseen. The need underneath is real, even when the way it comes out makes it hard to respond to.

Needs expressed clearly
What it looks like when regulated

Regulated requests

  • I am feeling disconnected from you. Can we spend some time together tonight?
  • I am feeling anxious and I could really use some reassurance right now.
  • I need to know we are okay. Can you tell me that?
  • I am struggling and I do not need you to fix it. I just need you close.
  • This conversation is feeling unsafe for me. Can we slow it down?
  • I need a hug before we keep talking.
A regulated request names the need directly and gives your partner something concrete they can respond to. It invites rather than demands.

Making a regulated request in four steps

  • 1.Regulate first. Make sure you are calm enough to speak without escalating. Pause, breathe, or take space if needed.
  • 2.Name the feeling. Use a single word: scared, lonely, anxious, sad. Not a story or an accusation.
  • 3.Name the need. Be specific: I need reassurance, I need connection, I need comfort.
  • 4.Make the request concrete. Tell your partner exactly what would help: a hug, to hear that you love me, to sit together for ten minutes.

The parenting parallel

Just as a young child needs comfort, reassurance, and connection from a trusted adult, these same needs stay with us into adulthood. They do not disappear. They go underground and surface in our closest relationships. Understanding this removes blame. Your partner is not the source of the need, but they are the person best placed to respond to it.
